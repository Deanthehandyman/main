How to Beat the East Texas "Pine Curtain"

If you live in Pittsburg, Gilmer, or anywhere in the woods, you know the struggle. You unbox your Starlink, set it on the lawn, and the app says "Obstructions." The trees are too tall, and the signal drops every 45 seconds.

The Solution: Elevation

Most DIY installs bolt the dish to the fascia. That works if you're in a field. In the woods, we need to go higher. I use industrial J-mounts and sometimes tower brackets to get that dish 10-15 feet above the roofline. Every foot of elevation gains you about 5 degrees of sky visibility.

Before I drill a single hole, I use the Starlink debug data to map the sky. I don't guess. If I can't find a spot with 0% obstructions, I'll tell you before we start.

Electrical Safety +
Why Your Outlet Feels Warm (And Why It's Dangerous)

I got a call last week from a homeowner in Mt. Pleasant. She said, "Dean, when I plug in my vacuum, the wall feels warm." That is a 911 call for a handyman.

The Culprit: "Backstabbed" Outlets

In many older homes, electricians pushed wires into the back of the outlet (held by a tiny spring) instead of wrapping them around the screw. Over time, that spring weakens. The wire gets loose. The electricity starts to jump (arc), creating heat.

How I Fix It

I don't just tighten it. I remove the outlet, cut the burnt wire tips, and install a commercial-grade outlet using the side-screw terminals. I torque it down so it never moves again.

Farm Tech +
Getting Internet to the Barn (Without Digging)

A customer in Broken Bow wanted Wi-Fi in his metal shop, 300 feet from the main house. He thought he had to rent a trencher and bury a cable.

The Better Way: Wireless Bridge

I installed a Point-to-Point (PTP) wireless bridge. Think of it as an invisible ethernet cable. We mount a small radio on the house and a receiver on the barn. They "talk" to each other perfectly.

The result? Full-speed internet in the barn for streaming music and running security cameras, with zero digging required.
